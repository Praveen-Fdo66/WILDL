Sinharaja Forest Reserve

Sinharaja Forest Reserve is a forest reserve in Sri Lanka that is also a biodiversity hotspot. It has international significance and has been declared by UNESCO as a Biosphere Reserve and World Heritage Site.(UNESCO World Heritage Centre, 2020)


Sinharaja is the country's last sustainable region of primary tropical rainforest, according to the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN). More over 60% of the trees are endemic, and many are endangered. 50% of Sri Lanka's indigenous animal species (particularly butterflies, amphibians, birds, snakes, and fish species). It is home to 95% of the world's indigenous birds.


Because of its inaccessibility, the steep virgin rainforest, which is part of the Sri Lanka lowland rain forests ecoregion, was protected from the worst of commercial logging and was proclaimed a World Biosphere Reserve in 1978 and a World Heritage Site in 1988.


Because of the lush foliage, wildlife is less visible than in dry-zone national parks like Yala. The endemic purple-faced langur is the most common bigger mammal.


The courageous Sri Lanka crested drongo and the boisterous orange-billed babbler lead the way in mixed feeding flocks. The 20 rainforest species, including the elusive red-faced malkoha, green-billed coucal, and Sri Lanka blue magpie, are all found here.


Reptiles include the indigenous green pit viper and hump-nosed viper, while amphibians, particularly tree frogs, are abundant. The endemic Sri Lankan birdwing butterfly and leeches are among the invertebrates.

Kumana National Park

Sri Lanka's Kumana National Park is famous for its avifauna, particularly vast flocks of migrating waterfowl and wading species. The park is located on Sri Lanka's southeastern coast, 391 kilometers (243 miles) southeast of Colombo.(de Livera, Lankika, 2005)Kumana National Park borders Yala National Park.(International Water Management Institute, 2006) Previously known as Yala East National Park, Kumana was renamed on September 5, 2006.(Mendis, Risidra, 2006)


Because of LTTE (Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam) attacks, the park was closed from 1985 until March 2003. It was also impacted by the 2004 Boxing Day tsunami.(Kariyawasam, Dayananda, 2005)

Physical Features

The national park's southern boundary is Kumbukkan Oya.(International Water Management Institute, 2006) The national park's considerable birdlife is supported by 20 lagoons and tanks.(Senarathna, P.M. 2009) The lagoons are shallow, with depths of less than two meters (6.6 feet). Kumana villu is occasionally inundated with ocean. The area's elevation ranges from sea level to 90 meters (300 feet). The average annual temperature is 27.30 °C (81.14 °F), while the yearly rainfall is 1,300 millimetres (51.18 in).
